The Role, Plain English

A Right-of-Way Agent is the person who gets the legal permission for a pipeline to cross private land — and manages the landowner relationship from first contact through construction and beyond. You are not a lawyer. You are not an engineer. You are the bridge between the company building the pipeline and the people who own the land it needs to cross.

What You Actually Do Every Day
Research Parcels
Before you ever knock on a door, you research who owns the land the pipeline needs to cross. This means pulling county records, reading deeds, checking for liens, identifying mortgages, and confirming who has signing authority. You build a parcel file before you approach anyone.
Contact Landowners
You are usually the first — and only — person from the company a landowner ever meets. First impressions matter enormously. You explain the project, answer questions honestly, and build enough trust to move toward a signed agreement. You don't rush this.
Negotiate Easements
You negotiate the terms of the easement — the legal right to cross the land — including compensation, ROW width, temporary construction workspace, damage payments, and any special conditions. You work within your company's approved parameters and bring anything non-standard to legal.
Manage Landowner Relations During Construction
After the easement is signed, your job isn't over. You keep landowners updated as construction happens. You handle complaints, coordinate damage assessments, and make sure crews are honoring what was promised.
Document Everything
ROW work is paper-intensive. Every contact, every offer, every counteroffer, every agreement gets documented. Your files support the legal team, title team, and surveyors. Sloppy records create project-level problems.
Who You Work With
Title Attorney / Title Team
They confirm legal ownership and clear title defects. You flag problems you find in research. They resolve them. You never attempt to resolve a title issue yourself.
Project Attorney
Reviews easement language, handles non-standard requests, and manages legal escalations. Any landowner request that deviates from the standard form goes through them first.
Surveyors
Establish legal boundaries. If there's a question about where a property line actually is — especially with older deeds — a surveyor resolves it. You don't estimate property boundaries.
Engineers & Construction Crews
You provide the constructible corridor — legal access to every parcel on the route. Construction cannot proceed on any parcel without a signed easement. You are the gatekeeper for that.
The Non-Negotiable Rules
You Are the Single Point of Contact
On most projects, you are the only company representative who communicates with landowners. Engineers, project managers, and legal teams do not contact landowners directly. This protects consistency and prevents conflicting messages.
No Construction on Unsigned Parcels
No activity — staking, clearing, grading, or pipe — occurs on any parcel without a signed easement. Schedule pressure from the construction side never overrides this. Trespass creates legal liability and destroys landowner trust.
You Identify Problems. Legal Resolves Them.
Your job is to find title issues, flag encumbrances, and document what you see. Deciding whether something matters legally is not your call. Attorneys make that determination. The faster you flag it, the better.
Honesty Is Not Optional
You don't make promises you can't keep, you don't misrepresent what the project does or doesn't do, and you don't use legal pressure as a negotiating tactic. Landowners talk to each other. Your reputation follows you through every county you work in.
Field Glossary
Ownership & Title
Fee Simple
The most complete form of land ownership. The owner holds the land outright, forever, with the right to sell, lease, or transfer it. When you run title on a parcel, this is the ownership type you're confirming.
Chain of Title
The complete history of ownership transfers on a parcel, going back in time. A clean chain means each owner properly received it from the last. A break in the chain — a missing deed, an unresolved estate — is a title defect that must be resolved before you can get an easement signed.
Deed
The legal document that transfers ownership from one party to another. For ROW work, you need the recorded deed to confirm who currently owns the land and in what form (individual, LLC, trust, etc.).
Grantor / Grantee
On a deed, the Grantor is the seller (the one giving away the property) and the Grantee is the buyer (the one receiving it). Deed indexes are organized by both — critical for running title at the courthouse.
Legal Description
The formal language in a deed that precisely describes the land's boundaries — using metes and bounds (bearings and distances), or the Public Land Survey System (townships, ranges, sections). You'll need to confirm your easement falls within the described parcel.
Tax Records / Parcel ID
In Ohio, property records are split across three county offices: the Recorder (deeds, mortgages, liens — the legal record), the Auditor (ownership, assessed value, parcel maps, sales history), and the Treasurer (tax billing and delinquencies). The Auditor's office is often your fastest first lookup — Ohio counties provide excellent free online access. But the Recorder's deed is what controls legal ownership. The tax roll is a starting point, not the answer.
Encumbrances & Interests
Easement
The legal right to use someone else's land for a specific purpose — like running a pipeline across it — without owning the land itself. This is the core deliverable of your job. Easements are recorded in the county records and run with the land.
Right-of-Way (ROW)
The strip of land (or the legal right to use that strip) for infrastructure like pipelines, power lines, or roads. The ROW easement defines the width, permitted uses, and duration. Permanent ROW stays with the pipeline forever; temporary construction ROW expires.
Lien
A legal claim against a property, usually for unpaid debt — a mortgage, unpaid taxes, a contractor's mechanics lien, or a court judgment. A property with a lien can still be easement-signed, but the lienholders may also have to consent. Always flag liens to your title attorney.
Mortgage
Ohio uses mortgages exclusively — not deeds of trust (which are common in Texas and other states). When a lender finances a property purchase, they record a mortgage with the County Recorder as security. Ohio requires judicial foreclosure through a sheriff's sale, which takes 7–12 months. On pipeline projects, lenders with recorded mortgages often must subordinate or consent to your easement. Flag every recorded mortgage to your title team — they handle the subordination process.
Mineral Rights
The rights to oil, gas, coal, and other resources beneath the surface. These can be owned separately from the surface. On a pipeline project, surface ownership controls your ROW, but mineral rights owners may have separate interests — especially if the pipeline crosses their mineral lease.
Encumbrance
Any claim, lien, easement, or restriction that affects a property's title or use. Running title means finding all encumbrances and understanding which ones affect your project.
Process & Field Terms
Title Search / Title Run
The process of examining public records to determine the current owner of a parcel, the chain of title, and any recorded encumbrances. On large pipeline projects, this is done for every parcel the route crosses — often hundreds.
Curative
The process of fixing title defects. If title research finds a break in the chain, an old unresolved heir, or an improperly released lien, curative work is required before the easement can be finalized. This often requires attorneys and can take weeks or months.
Eminent Domain / Condemnation
The legal right of certain companies to take private land for public use with just compensation. In Ohio, this is called "appropriation" and is governed by ORC Chapter 163. Interstate pipelines may use federal authority under the Natural Gas Act; intrastate pipelines in Ohio must qualify under ORC 1723.01 or related statutes — and whether a specific pipeline qualifies is sometimes contested. Not all pipeline companies have this authority. It is always a last resort, always involves attorneys and county courts, and is never a negotiating tool. In Ohio, landowners have a constitutional right to challenge necessity and to a jury trial on compensation.
Just Compensation
The fair market value payment owed to a landowner when an easement is taken. In Ohio, the state constitution (Article I, Section 19) gives landowners the right to have just compensation assessed by a jury — stronger than the federal standard. This is why Ohio landowners often have real leverage in condemnation proceedings and why negotiated agreements are almost always preferable. Your company's appraiser establishes the baseline offer, but the landowner can request a jury trial on the amount.
Damage Payments
Compensation paid beyond the easement value — for crop damage, timber removal, fence replacement, or disruption to operations. These are negotiated separately and must be documented carefully.
Subordination Agreement
A lender's consent to allow a pipeline easement to have priority over their mortgage — so the easement survives if the property is foreclosed on. Your company's legal team typically handles obtaining these.
Ohio-Specific Instruments
Survivorship Deed
Ohio has a statutory survivorship deed form (ORC 5302.17) — "to A and B for their joint lives, remainder to the survivor." This is extremely common for Ohio farm properties held by married couples. When one owner dies, the survivor takes the whole interest automatically without probate. In ROW work: if one spouse has died and the property was held in survivorship, the surviving spouse is typically the sole owner and the only signature required — but you should verify with your title attorney and may need a death certificate filed at the county.
Dower Rights
Ohio is one of the few remaining states that recognizes dower rights (ORC 2103.02). A married person's spouse holds a potential 1/3 life interest in real property acquired during the marriage. This means that even if a deed shows only one spouse as owner, the other spouse may need to sign an easement to release their dower interest. This comes up constantly in Ohio ROW work — always check marital status and flag it to your title attorney if the signing owner is or was married.
Transfer-on-Death (TOD) Affidavit
Ohio allows property owners to record a TOD affidavit (ORC 5302.22) designating a beneficiary who automatically receives the property on death — without probate. The owner retains full control during their lifetime and can revoke or change it. In ROW work: if you find a recorded TOD affidavit, note it — the owner can still sign your easement, but the beneficiary designation may affect future title if the owner dies during or after the project. Flag it to your title attorney.
Ohio Auditor / Three-Office System
Ohio property records are split across three county offices. The Recorder holds deeds, mortgages, and liens — this is the legal record of title. The Auditor holds ownership data, assessed values, parcel maps, and sales history — often the fastest starting point online. The Treasurer holds tax billing and delinquency records. For ROW work: start with the Auditor for parcel identification and ownership leads, then verify and run title through the Recorder. All Ohio counties provide free online access to both Auditor and Recorder records.

How This Works
What this app is
A scenario-based training system for someone preparing to become a Right-of-Way Agent in the oil and gas pipeline industry, built with Ohio-specific law and practice. It teaches through decisions, not lectures — every scenario puts you in a real situation and asks what you would do.
The three decks
Deck 01 (Into the Field) covers the role itself — duties, conduct, and judgment. Deck 02 (Title & Legal) covers titles, deeds, liens, easements, and Ohio property law. Deck 03 (Advanced Field) covers permits, regulatory dependencies, damage settlement, and complex situations. Do them in order the first time.
How scoring works
Each deck tracks three skill axes starting at 50. Good choices raise them; poor choices lower them. Each run draws 10 random scenarios from that deck's pool, so runs differ. Your best score and run count are saved on this device and shown on each deck card.
Wrong answers are the point
The feedback on a wrong answer teaches more than the feedback on a right one. Read both. When you miss a scenario, the correct choice is highlighted so you can see the reasoning gap.
Reference tabs
The Role Guide is a plain-English breakdown of what the job actually is — read it before your first run of Deck 01. The Field Glossary defines 20 terms, including four Ohio-specific instruments (dower rights, survivorship deeds, TOD affidavits, and the three-office records system) that come up constantly in Ohio ROW work.
Common Questions
Do my scores go anywhere?
No. Everything stays on this device in local browser storage. Nothing is transmitted, tracked, or shared. Clearing your browser data resets your progress.
Why do the same scenarios repeat?
Each deck draws 10 of its scenarios per run (Deck 01 has 23, Deck 02 has 23, Deck 03 has 13). Repeats become more common after several runs. When a repeat appears, use it — try to articulate the rule before tapping.
Is this legal advice?
No. This is a training aid built for interview preparation and foundational learning. Real ROW work follows your employer's procedures, your project attorneys, and current law. When this app and your future employer's training disagree, the employer wins.
What should I know for an interview?
The patterns matter more than the details: verify before you act, escalate legal questions to attorneys, document everything, never let schedule pressure override property rights, and treat every landowner honestly. If you can explain WHY each of those rules exists, you are ahead of most entry-level candidates.
