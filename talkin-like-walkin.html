A Cadence Trainer

Talkin'·Like·Walkin'

You don't copy the voice. You copy the punctuation he invented — the pauses where you'd never pause, the stress on the wrong word, the flat line that suddenly jumps. Your mic checks the shape. The dots are where you stop.

The Method

Six things make the impression. Learn them one at a time. None of them is the voice.

·

One — The pause goes where you'd keep going

Not at the comma. Mid-clause. Right before a small, unimportant word. He learned it in Astoria, hearing immigrant neighbors stop to find the English word — a staccato rhythm he kept on purpose.

I had a goldfish · once · it taught me everything I know · about patience

!

Two — Stress the word nobody stresses

Lean hard on an article, a preposition, a throwaway. The emphasis lands somewhere the meaning didn't ask for.

There are two kinds of people · and I am neither of them

—

Three — Flat, then a jump

Near-monotone for a stretch — almost robotic — then a sudden pitch spike on one word, then flat again. Limited variation makes the spikes hit.

···

Four — Staccato bursts, often in threes

Short bunches of words, broken cleanly, frequently grouped three at a time. Theatrical phrasing from his musical-stage years.

▲▼

Five — Wide dynamic range

Booming to near-whisper inside one line. Commit to the swing; don't deliver everything at one volume.

?

Six — Delete the punctuation, write your own

His master trick: strip every mark from the script and re-author it. A statement becomes a question. A question becomes a statement. The flow stops being the writer's and starts being his. That's what the Walken-izer does to any sentence you give it.

How to use this: Calibrate your mic once, then drill 03–05 a few minutes each. The mic measures rhythm and pitch shape — not whether you nailed a specific word — so trust your ear alongside the numbers. The Pause

Speak the line out loud and actually stop at every dot — the long one (··) is a held beat. The mic doesn't read your words; it reads your silences. It's checking that you broke the line into staccato bursts instead of reading it smooth.

The Lift

One word is marked. Say the line and punch that word — louder, or sharper — then drop back. The mic measures your dynamic range: did you actually commit to a swing, or stay flat?
