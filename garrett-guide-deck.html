Quick Reference
The rules that don't change, no matter the situation.
🌋
When he's flooding (meltdown in progress)
▼
Words don't land right now. Logic, explanations, "why did you do that" — his brain can't process them. Save it for later.
Lower your voice, slow your body. His nervous system is mirroring yours. Calm outside helps calm inside.
If he's circular-arguing (same points on repeat, no resolution): say "I can see we're both stuck. I'm going to give us some space." Then actually leave. Not punishment — reset.
The last trigger is rarely the real cause. Ask yourself: what happened before this? The stack is the issue, not just the final hit.
Big language (death, hate, terrible mom) = enormous feelings with no better words. Don't argue the content. Validate the size. Route to Mom or Dad.
🧊
After it passes (the calm after the storm)
▼
Wait until he's fully calm — genuinely calm. Not "quiet because he's still activated." The difference is visible.
Repair, not review. Open with warmth, not "now let's talk about what happened." He already knows. He feels shame about it.
One specific true thing beats general reassurance. Not "you're so smart." Try: "You figured out how to get Mismageus into the Outer Rings. You did that."
Collaborative problem solving works here. "That was really hard. Can we figure out together what might help next time?" His own solutions have much higher compliance than imposed ones.
🚫
Things that make it worse — always
▼
Asking the same thing more than once. Each repeat stacks as a threat to his autonomy. Ask once, then wait.
Helping anyway when he said no. Offer once, neutrally. Back off completely. Let him come to you.
Consequences during flooding. He cannot process them. They escalate. Wait until he's calm.
Long explanations of why he's wrong. His brain is full. Short, calm, one thing.
Escalating power struggles. He will always escalate further. The exit is yours to model.
🧰
Regulation tools that actually work
▼
Breath game: "Smell the pizza, blow out the candles." Playful framing keeps it accessible.
Frustration bucket: "How full is your bucket right now — like 1 to 10?" Helps him name it.
Heavy work: Push palms together hard. Stomp feet. Push against a wall. His body needs the input.
First/then: "First one quick thing, then Kirby." Makes the demand visible and bounded.
Genuine laughter interrupts spirals better than any technique. Use it when you can find it.
Feeling at a distance: Route emotional questions through his worlds. "What do you think makes Bay-bay feel scared?" instead of asking him directly.
💜
For aunts visiting — what to know first
▼
Two exciting things on the same day = collapse risk. Library + your visit + a shower before leaving = too much stacked. Know this going in.
He agreed to seeing you and he means it. When he fights it right before you arrive, that's not about you — it's that the plan became real and real plans have variables he can't control.
Give him micro-choices: "When I get there, do you want to show me Treat Land first, or something else?" Gives him control over a small piece.
His creatures and worlds are real to him. Ask about them with genuine curiosity. Ask who lives in Outer Rings. Ask what Mismageus sounds like. He will light up.
Bay-bay is his plushie dinosaur and she matters deeply. Don't tease it. Ask about her if he mentions her.
If he melts down during your visit, it is not your fault. Follow his parents' lead. You being calm is the best help you can give.
