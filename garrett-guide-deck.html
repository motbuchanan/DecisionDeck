Before You Arrive

A quick read of his day. Takes 60 seconds. Changes everything.

Check off anything that's already happened today. The more boxes checked, the less runway he has — adjust your expectations accordingly.

These aren't predictions. They're inputs. Knowing the load helps you carry less of it.

Sleep & morning
✓
Sleep was rough or short
Low sleep = low tolerance for everything. Expect faster escalation.
✓
Morning was already hard
An argument, a meltdown, or a rough start means the tank is already drained.
Food & body
✓
He hasn't eaten much today
Hungry = lower threshold. Make sure something he likes is available.
✓
He's had outdoor or physical time
Movement helps regulate. A kid who's been inside all day has more stored energy.
Social & sensory load
✓
There was already a transition or disruption today
A plan that changed, an errand, a schedule shift — these all cost something.
✓
He's already had social time today
Social output is expensive for him. Your visit may be his second event of the day.
✓
Something exciting already happened today
Two exciting things in one day is a known collapse pattern. Know this going in.
Right now
✓
He's in the middle of something (game, project, video)
An interrupted open loop will follow him into your visit. Give him time to reach a stopping point.
✓
He seems irritable or activated right now
Something is already loading. Low demands, no big entrance, let him come to you.

Something Just Happened

He's in it right now. Here's exactly what to do.

Don't read this whole page. Just do the steps in order. Come back and read the why later.
During — right now
1
Stop talking
Every word you add is more load on a system that's already full. Silence is not abandonment — it's the first de-escalation move.
2
Lower your body and your voice
His nervous system mirrors yours. Slow, low, calm outside helps calm inside. Crouch if you can. Sit down.
3
Stay nearby but don't pursue
Physical presence without pressure tells him the relationship is intact. Don't follow him if he moves away — but don't disappear either.
4
Wait. Actually wait.
This will pass. It always passes. Your job right now is not to fix it — it's to not make it worse. That means waiting longer than feels comfortable.
5
If you need to leave the room, name it first
"I'm going to give us both some space. I'm right here when you're ready." Then go to the next room. Stay in earshot. Don't slam anything.
— when he's calm again —
After — once it's passed
6
Don't process it immediately
He's calm but not recovered. Moving straight to "let's talk about what happened" can reopen it. Give it real time — 20 minutes minimum.
7
If he comes to you sweet and close, receive it
That's a repair bid. He's reaching toward you. Receive it warmly — no lesson attached, no reference to what just happened. Just warmth.
8
One specific true thing, said quietly
Not "you're such a good kid." Find something real and recent. Something he made, discovered, or did. Say it once. Let it land.
Never during a meltdown
Explain why he was wrong
Issue a consequence
Ask him why he did it
Tell him to calm down
Bring up what he said in anger
Make him apologize before he's ready
